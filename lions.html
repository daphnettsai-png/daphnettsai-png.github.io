Lions-Med

Developed in collaboration with Yuchen Guo, Aryan Kaul, and Ana Vizitiv.

Lions-Med is a disaster healthcare access app designed to help people find reliable clinic information during emergencies, especially when they are in the medical gray zone: not sick enough for the ER, but still needing care.

Open Prototype Back to Portfolio

The Problem

During disasters, healthcare access becomes confusing and unreliable. Clinics may close, hours may change, transportation may be disrupted, and online information may be outdated. This especially hurts people whose medical needs are important but not emergency-level.

Medical Gray Zone

Lions-Med focuses on people who need care but may not belong in the ER, such as someone missing routine treatment, follow-up care, or non-emergency medical support during a disaster.

Information Gap

The core issue is not only whether care exists, but whether people can trust that the information they see is accurate and current.

The Solution

Lions-Med uses community-driven updates, clinic information, and verification signals to help users understand which clinics are open, what services are available, and whether the information is trustworthy.

Live Clinic Updates

Users can view nearby clinics and see whether they are open, closed, or recently updated.

Community Reporting

People in the community can submit updates, helping the platform stay current during fast-changing disaster situations.

Verification

Updates can be checked through multiple signals so users are not relying on one random post or outdated website.

Privacy Awareness

The app is designed around sharing clinic status information without requiring users to expose unnecessary personal medical details.

Use Case

Imagine a Columbia student during a flood warning who is experiencing a medical issue related to a recent surgery. It is not an ER-level emergency, but they still need care. Instead of walking to a clinic that turns out to be closed, they can check Lions-Med for recent updates and find a more reliable care option.

Ideation & Iteration

Our original idea focused on healthcare access during a disaster, but through feedback we realized we needed to make the disaster context clearer and focus more specifically on people whose medical needs fall between routine care and emergency care.

Early Direction

We started with the idea of helping users find open clinics when normal systems are disrupted.

Feedback

We refined the concept to emphasize the medical gray zone, community engagement, transparency, and privacy-aware crowdsourcing.

Final Focus

The final product centers on trustworthy, real-time clinic information during any disaster, not only one specific type of crisis.

Community Angle

We chose a community-based model because people are more likely to contribute useful updates when the tool is built around a group they trust.
